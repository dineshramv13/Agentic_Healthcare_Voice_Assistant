# Prescriptions

## Repeat Prescriptions

Patients on long-term medication can order repeat prescriptions in three ways:

1. **Online** through the patient portal — select "Order Repeat Prescription", choose the medications needed from your repeat list, and submit. Online orders are the fastest method.
2. **By phone**, calling the surgery's dedicated prescription line (separate from the main appointment line) between 9:00 AM and 4:00 PM, Monday to Friday.
3. **By dropping off a paper request slip** at the reception desk. Paper slips are the slowest method and are discouraged unless the patient cannot use the other two options.

## Processing Timelines

Repeat prescriptions normally take **2 working days** to process after the request is submitted. Patients should request a repeat **at least 5 working days before** their current supply runs out to allow for processing, pharmacy preparation, and possible GP review. Requests submitted on a Friday are typically processed by Tuesday due to the weekend.

## Pharmacy Collection

Once processed, the prescription is sent electronically to the patient's nominated pharmacy (set up when registering with the surgery). Patients can change their nominated pharmacy at any time by contacting reception. Collection from the pharmacy itself depends on that pharmacy's opening hours and stock availability — the surgery cannot guarantee a specific medication is in stock at the pharmacy.

## Medication Reviews

Certain medications (controlled substances, blood pressure medication, mental health medication) require a periodic medication review before a repeat can be approved. If a review is due, the system will flag the prescription request and a GP or pharmacist will contact the patient to book a short telephone review, usually within 3 working days.

## Lost or Stolen Medication

If medication is lost, stolen, or damaged, the surgery cannot simply reissue a duplicate prescription early in most cases. The patient should contact the surgery to discuss the circumstances; a GP will use clinical judgement on whether an early reissue is appropriate, particularly for essential medications (e.g. insulin, epilepsy medication, heart medication).

## New Prescriptions (Non-Repeat)

A new medication that is not already on a patient's repeat list requires a GP consultation first, either in person or via telephone consultation. New prescriptions cannot be requested directly online without a prior consultation, except in specific nurse-led pathways (e.g. contraception reviews).

## Side Effects and Urgent Concerns

If a patient experiences a suspected adverse reaction or side effect, they should not simply stop the medication without guidance for medications where abrupt stopping can be harmful (e.g. some blood pressure or mental health medications). They should contact the surgery for urgent advice, or NHS 111 if outside surgery hours and the reaction is not severe enough for 999.
