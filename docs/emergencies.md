# Emergencies — When to Call 999 vs NHS 111 vs Book a GP

## Call 999 Immediately If

- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness, especially spreading to the arm, neck, or jaw
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath that is severe or sudden
- Signs of a stroke: face drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech (FAST test)
- Severe bleeding that cannot be stopped
- Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
- Severe allergic reaction (swelling of face/throat, difficulty breathing after exposure to an allergen)
- Suspected heart attack or cardiac arrest
- Severe burns
- Suspected major fracture, especially with visible deformity
- Choking where the airway is blocked
- A seizure that lasts longer than 5 minutes, or repeated seizures
- Any situation where you believe a life is at immediate risk

999 is for life-threatening emergencies only. Ambulance response is prioritised by severity, so non-emergency calls can delay care for patients in genuine danger.

## Call NHS 111 If

- The problem is urgent but not life-threatening
- You are not sure which NHS service you need
- You need advice on what to do next for a worsening but stable condition
- You need an out-of-hours GP appointment when the surgery is closed
- A child has a persistent high fever that is concerning but the child is otherwise alert and responsive
- You need urgent mental health support and are not in immediate danger

NHS 111 is available 24/7, by phone or online, and can dispatch an ambulance itself if the situation turns out to be more serious than initially described.

## Book a Routine or Same-Day GP Appointment If

- Symptoms have been present for several days without improvement
- You need a medication review or repeat prescription issue resolved
- You have a non-urgent but bothersome symptom (skin issue, ongoing mild pain, routine check-up)
- You need a letter, referral, or form filled in by a GP

## A Note on This System's Limits

This assistant is **not** a substitute for emergency services and does not make clinical diagnoses. If a user describes anything resembling the 999 list above, this system's role is solely to immediately direct them to call 999 (or 112) and, where relevant, to stay on the line and follow the operator's instructions. The assistant must never attempt to talk a user through a medical emergency itself, delay the redirect with follow-up questions, or suggest waiting to see if symptoms improve.
